Presentations at KI2025 Doctoral Consortium

  1. Home
  2. Student Progress

At this year’s KI2025 conference, three of our doctoral researchers—Carolin Cissée, Souptik Sen, and Abeer Mostafa—took part in the Doctoral Consortium, presenting their research topics and engaging with leading experts in the field of Artificial Intelligence. Carolin Cissée presented her PhD research topic, “Data-Efficient Multimodal Training Strategies,” outlining the core ideas and planned workflow of her project. She received valuable feedback from both the audience and her DC mentor, Prof. Dr. Felix Weitkämper, particularly on the challenges of handling multimodal data and the general hurdles young researchers often encounter. Carolin returned from the conference with new impressions, fresh ideas, and meaningful connections to other early-career researchers. Souptik Sen showcased his work on “Model Efficiency Techniques in Multimodal Learning,” which addresses the critical challenge of reducing computational complexity when processing diverse data types such as video, audio, and text. His talk emphasized the need for unified frameworks that balance efficiency with performance—an area where current research is still lacking. His discussions with his DC mentor, Prof. Dr. Frieder Stolzenburg, centered on refining his approach toward building generalized architectures that can adapt across tasks and datasets, while also exploring promising directions for future work. Abeer Mostafa presented her PhD proposal, “Efficient Graph-Based Neural Architectures for Multimodal Learning.” In her presentation, she explored how graph neural networks can play a pivotal role in advancing multimodal research. Through discussions with her DC mentor, Prof. Dr. Tom Hanika, Abeer gained valuable insights into potential directions for shaping her doctoral work and contributing to the future of graph-based approaches in AI. Beyond the doctoral consortium, KI2025 featured inspiring talks on multimodality, large language models, and explainable AI, providing an excellent opportunity for our students to learn, network, and exchange ideas with researchers from around the world. Their participation reflects not only the progress of their individual projects but also the vibrancy of our community’s contributions to the AI research landscape.
